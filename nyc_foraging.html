Yes, you can find some of your groceries in New York City nature.

Discovering wild edibles in America's largest city

← Back to Portfolio

Every weekend, Steve "Wildman" Brill makes his trek out to one of New York City's parks to teach intrigued beginners how to forage in an urban setting. Touring residents and visitors since the 1980s, there is no doubt that he has taught thousands of people how to safely eat and cook with plants scattered around the small amount of greenery that the city has. On a rainy Saturday morning in April, around 20 people showed up to learn from the Wildman in Prospect Park, Brooklyn. Things are much different than the 80s, where Brill was arrested by undercover park rangers for eating dandelion in Central Park. Although foraging is banned in the city's parks, his dandelion-eating arrest decades ago now keeps the rangers off his tail. And despite the downpour, Brill still had an audience. New York City is known as one of the most iconic cities in the world, although many probably wouldn't consider it as a place of nature. Yet, hidden inside certain pockets in the city and its parks lie places for residents to forage– a scavenger hunt for food and provisions growing.

Mulberries, mugwort, grape leaves: All can be found in New York City, scattered throughout the boroughs.

Spring Treasures

In spring, New York City's parks and green spaces come alive with edible plants like dandelion greens, wild garlic, and garlic mustard. These early risers are packed with nutrients and can be found in places like Central Park and Prospect Park.

Foragers eagerly anticipate the appearance of ramps (wild leeks) and morel mushrooms in more wooded areas of the city.

Summer Bounty

Summer brings an abundance of berries throughout the city. Mulberries stain sidewalks purple in June, while black raspberries and wineberries can be found along park edges and wooded trails.

This is also the season for juneberries (serviceberries), which resemble blueberries and can be found in many city parks and street plantings.

Fall Harvest

Fall is perhaps the richest season for urban foraging. Wild apples, pears, and even persimmons can be found in parks and along streets. Hardy greens like lambsquarters continue to thrive.

The brave and knowledgeable might seek out hen-of-the-woods and chicken-of-the-woods mushrooms that appear on trees and stumps after rainfall.

Winter Findings

Even in winter, foraging continues. Rose hips from wild roses provide vitamin C, while certain nuts that fell in autumn may still be gathered from the ground.

Pine needles can be harvested to make tea rich in vitamin C, and the inner bark of certain trees can be used in survival situations.

Some of the rules of the trade include axing any greedy tendencies by always leaving more for others, avoiding poisonous plants and possibly-poisonous areas, and cleaning finds before they're eaten.

Central Park

Central Park offers some of the most diverse foraging in Manhattan, with mulberry trees, blackberries, and various herbs found throughout its 840 acres.

"I've been leading tours through Central Park for over 20 years," says Steve 'Wildman' Brill. "The north woods section has some of the city's best wild edibles."

Prospect Park

Brooklyn's Prospect Park is another foraging hotspot, particularly along its less-manicured edges where wild berries, nuts, and mushrooms grow in abundance.

The park's diverse ecosystem supports dozens of edible species throughout the year.

Inwood Hill Park

Manhattan's last natural forest, Inwood Hill Park provides a more wild environment where mushrooms, berries, and native plants grow with less human intervention.

The ancient forest here offers some of the most authentic foraging experiences in the city.

Pelham Bay Park

The Bronx's Pelham Bay Park—the city's largest park at over 2,700 acres—features beaches, forests, and meadows where a wide variety of edibles can be found year-round.

Its diverse habitats support everything from beach plums to wild grapes.

Safety First

While foraging in New York City can be rewarding, it's important to follow some basic guidelines:

  • Never eat anything you cannot identify with 100% certainty
  • Be aware of local regulations (some parks prohibit foraging)
  • Avoid plants growing near roads or in potentially contaminated soil
  • Take only what you need and leave plenty for others and wildlife
  • Clean all foraged foods thoroughly before consuming
  • Consider joining a guided tour for beginners

"The city offers a bounty of wild edibles for those who know where to look." - Steve 'Wildman' Brill
