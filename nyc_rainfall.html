Historical Rainfall in NYC (1869-Present)

A data visualization exploring over 150 years of precipitation patterns in New York City

About This Project

This visualization tracks New York City's rainfall patterns from 1869 to the present day, revealing how precipitation has changed over more than 150 years. The heatmap format allows us to see seasonal patterns, yearly variations, and long-term climate trends that might otherwise be difficult to perceive in traditional graphs.

Each cell represents a specific month's rainfall amount, with color intensity indicating precipitation levels. Darker colors represent higher rainfall, while lighter colors show drier periods. This visualization helps identify historical droughts, unusually wet years, and shifting seasonal patterns that may be linked to climate change.

Key Insights

  • The data reveals several multi-year drought periods throughout NYC's history, notably in the 1960s and early 2000s.
  • Recent decades show an increase in extreme precipitation events, with more intense rainfall concentrated in shorter periods.
  • Seasonal rainfall patterns have shifted slightly over time, with wetter springs and drier late summers becoming more common.
  • The visualization highlights the cyclical nature of precipitation patterns, while also revealing subtle long-term trends that may be attributed to climate change.

Methodology

This visualization uses historical weather data from the National Weather Service and NOAA, collected at Central Park's weather station—one of the longest continuously operating weather stations in the United States. Monthly precipitation totals were compiled, cleaned, and visualized using Flourish Studio's heatmap visualization tool.

Data cleaning involved addressing a small number of missing values by using averages from adjacent years and cross-referencing with secondary sources. The color scale was carefully calibrated to highlight both subtle variations in normal rainfall amounts and extreme precipitation events.
